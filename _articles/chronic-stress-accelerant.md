## Stress isn't just how you feel

The way most people think about stress is subjective: you either feel stressed, or you don't. The biology is more precise — and more concerning.

Chronic psychological stress produces measurable changes at the cellular level that accelerate biological ageing by years, not months. The mechanisms are well-characterised, the effects are cumulative, and the threshold for harm is lower than most people assume.

## Telomere attrition

Telomeres are the protective caps at the end of chromosomes that shorten each time a cell divides. Telomere length is one of the most validated biomarkers of biological age — cells with shorter telomeres divide less effectively, produce more inflammatory signals, and eventually stop dividing altogether (cellular senescence).

Studies consistently show that individuals with chronically elevated psychological stress have shorter telomeres than age-matched controls. The effect is dose-dependent: more sustained stress, greater attrition. Caregivers of chronically ill family members — one of the most intensely studied high-stress populations — show telomere shortening equivalent to roughly a decade of additional biological ageing.

## The cortisol axis

The primary mediator of the stress response is cortisol — a glucocorticoid hormone secreted by the adrenal glands in response to perceived threat. In acute doses, cortisol is adaptive: it mobilises energy, suppresses non-essential functions, and prepares the body for action.

Chronically elevated cortisol disrupts:

- **Sleep architecture** — particularly slow-wave sleep, which requires cortisol to be at its daily nadir
- **Immune regulation** — initially immunosuppressive, chronic cortisol exposure leads to immune dysregulation and increased inflammation
- **Metabolic function** — cortisol promotes visceral fat accumulation and impairs insulin sensitivity
- **Hippocampal volume** — sustained cortisol exposure is associated with reduced grey matter in the hippocampus, affecting memory and emotional regulation
- **Magnesium depletion** — cortisol mobilises magnesium from tissue stores, increasing urinary excretion

## Inflammation as the common pathway

Many of the long-term effects of chronic stress converge on a single mechanism: low-grade systemic inflammation. Elevated inflammatory markers (particularly IL-6 and CRP) are consistently associated with:

- Cardiovascular disease progression
- Accelerated cognitive decline
- Type 2 diabetes risk
- Certain cancer types

The relationship between psychological stress and inflammation is bidirectional — stress promotes inflammation, and inflammation amplifies stress responses.

## What the evidence supports

No supplement reverses chronic stress. What nutritional science can offer is support for the physiological systems most depleted by it:

**Magnesium** — the most directly relevant micronutrient. Cortisol actively depletes magnesium stores; stress and magnesium insufficiency create a reinforcing cycle. Magnesium contributes to a reduction of tiredness and fatigue and to normal functioning of the nervous system.

**B vitamins** — support the energy-yielding metabolism that stress disrupts. Individual B vitamins contribute to normal energy-yielding metabolism.

**Vitamin D** — deficiency is independently associated with depressive symptoms and dysregulated stress responses. Vitamins D and K contribute to the maintenance of normal bones, and D has broader immune-regulatory effects.

## The honest position

Scōp is not a stress solution. The interventions with the strongest evidence for stress reduction are behavioural: sleep, exercise, social connection, and for many people, psychological support.

What Scōp provides is a nutritional floor — ensuring that the micronutrient systems most vulnerable to chronic stress are consistently supported, regardless of how the rest of the day goes.

That's a meaningful contribution. It's just not the whole answer.
